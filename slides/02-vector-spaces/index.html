2.1 Definition and examples

Before we can define vectors spaces, we must define .

Scalars are just any element in a field.

Most of the time, if you want to keep things simple, you can think of scalars as being .

Definition 1 (Field.). A field is a set \(F\) with two binary operations \(F\times F \to F\) \[\begin{aligned} (\alpha,\beta) & \mapsto \alpha+\beta & (\alpha,\beta) & \mapsto \alpha\beta \end{aligned}\] called addition and multiplication such that always

  1. \(\alpha + \beta = \beta + \alpha\)  \((\alpha + \beta) + \gamma = \alpha + (\beta + \gamma)\)

  2. there exists \(0 \in F\) with \(\alpha + 0 = \alpha\)

  3. each \(\alpha \in F\) has a negative \(-\alpha\) with \(\alpha + (-\alpha) = 0\)

  4. \(\alpha \beta = \beta \alpha\)  \((\alpha \beta) \gamma = \alpha (\beta \gamma)\)

  5. \(\alpha (\beta + \gamma) = \alpha \beta + \alpha \gamma\)

  6. there exists \(1 \in F\) with \(1 \neq 0\) and \(1\alpha = \alpha\)

  7. each \(\alpha \in F\) with \(\alpha \neq 0\) has \(\alpha^{-1}\) (or \(1/\alpha\)) with \(\alpha \alpha^{-1} = 1\).

Example 2 (Examples of fields.).

  1. \(\mathbb{Q}= \{\, m/n \mid m,n \in \mathbb{Z}, \; n \neq 0 \,\}\) (the rationals)

  2. \(\mathbb{R}\) (the reals)

  3. \(\mathbb{C}= \{\, x+iy \mid x,y \in \mathbb{R}\,\}\) (the complex numbers)

  4. \(\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}= \{ 0,1,\dots,p-1 \}\), where \(p\) is a prime number, with addition and multiplication being performed modulo \(p\).

Abstract vectors

Definition 3 (Vector space.). Let \(F\) be a field. A vector space over \(F\) is a set \(V\) with operations \[\begin{aligned} V \times V & \to V & F \times V & \to V \\ (u,v) & \mapsto u+v & (\alpha,v) & \mapsto \alpha v \end{aligned}\] called addition and scalar multiplication such that always

  1. \(u + v = v + u\) \((u + v) + w = u + (v + w)\)

  2. there is a vector \(\vec{0}\) with \(v + \vec{0} = v\)

  3. each vector \(v\) has a negative \(-v\) with \(v + (-v) = \vec{0}\)

  4. \(\alpha (u + v) = \alpha u + \alpha v\)

  5. \((\alpha + \beta)v = \alpha v + \beta v\)

  6. \((\alpha \beta)v = \alpha (\beta v)\)

  7. \(1 v = v\).

Example ..: Standard examples (i)

Let \(F\) be a field and define \[\alert{F^{n}} = \left\{ \begin{pmatrix} x_{1} \\ x_{2} \\ \vdots \\ x_{n} \end{pmatrix} \;\middle|\; x_{i} \in F \right\}.\]

Vector space operations: \[\begin{pmatrix} x_{1} \\ x_{2} \\ \vdots \\ x_{n} \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} y_{1} \\ y_{2} \\ \vdots \\ y_{n} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} x_{1}+y_{1} \\ x_{2}+y_{2} \\ \vdots \\ x_{n}+y_{n}\end{pmatrix}, \qquad \pause \alpha \begin{pmatrix} x_{1} \\ x_{2} \\ \vdots \\ x_{n}\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \alpha x_{1} \\ \alpha x_{2} \\ \vdots \\ \alpha x_{n}\end{pmatrix}\]

Example ..: Standard examples (ii)

The complex numbers \(\mathbb{C}\) form a vector space over \(\mathbb{R}\).

Vector space operations: \[(x_{1} + iy_{1}) + (x_{2}+iy_{2}) = (x_{1}+x_{2}) + i(y_{1}+y_{2})\] \[\alpha (x + iy) = (\alpha x) + i(\alpha y) \quad \text{for $\alpha \in \mathbb{R}$}.\]

Example ..: Standard examples (iii)

\(F[x]\): the set of all polynomials \[f(x) = a_{0} + a_{1}x + a_{2}x^{2} + \dots + a_{m}x^{m}\] where \(a_{0},a_{1},\dots,a_{m} \in F\).

Vector space operations: If \(f(x) = \sum a_{i}x^{i}\) and \(g(x) = \sum b_{i}x^{i}\), then \[f(x) + g(x) = \sum_{i} (a_{i}+b_{i})x^{i}\] \[\alpha f(x) = \sum_{i} (\alpha a_{i})x^{i}\]

Example ..: Standard examples (iv)

\(\mathcal{F}_{\mathbb{R}}\): the set of all functions \(f \colon \mathbb{R}\to \mathbb{R}\)

Vector space operations: \[(f+g)(x) = f(x) + g(x)\] \[(\alpha f)(x) = \alpha \cdot f(x)\] \[(-f)(x) = -f(x)\] \[\vec{0} \colon x \mapsto 0 \quad \text{for all $x \in \mathbb{R}$}\]

Some important points

  • The zero of a field is denoted \(0\).

  • The zero vector will always be denoted by \(\vec{0}\).

  • \(0\) and \(\vec{0}\) are almost never equal, so beware!

  • We write \(\vec{v} \in F ^ n\) to represent column vectors, and \(v\in V\) to represent (abstract) vectors.

Basic properties of vector spaces

Proposition 4 (cf. MT2501). Let \(V\) be a vector space over a field \(F\), let \(v \in V\), and let \(\alpha \in F\). Then

  1. \(\alpha \vec{0} = \vec{0}\)

  2. \(0 v = \vec{0}\)

  3. if \(\alpha v = \vec{0}\), either \(\alpha = 0\) or \(v = \vec{0}\)

  4. \((-\alpha)v = -\alpha v = \alpha(-v)\).

The subspace criteria

Theorem 7 (Subspace criteria (cf. MT2501).). Let \(V\) be a vector space over a field \(F\) and let \(W\) be a subset of \(V\). Then \(W\) is a subspace of \(V\) if and only if

  1. \(W\) is non-empty;

  2. if \(u,v \in W\), then \(u+v \in W\); and

  3. if \(v \in W\) and \(\alpha \in F\), then \(\alpha v \in W\).

Spanning sets

. ..

Definition 11 (Linear combination, span). Let \(V\) be a vector space over a field \(F\) and let \(\mathscr{A}= \{ v_{1}, v_{2}, \dots\}\subset V\). A linear combination of vectors in \(\mathscr{A}\) is: \[\sum_{i=1}^{k} \alpha_{i}v_{i} = \alpha_{1}v_{1} + \alpha_{2}v_{2} + \dots + \alpha_{k}v_{k}\] where \(\alpha_{1},\alpha_{2},\dots,\alpha_{k} \in F\). The set \[\begin{aligned} \operatorname{Span}(\mathscr{A}) & = & \operatorname{Span}(v_{1},v_{2}, \dots) \\ & = & \biggl\{ \sum_{i=1}^{k} \alpha_{i}v_{i} \biggm| v_{1},v_{2},\dots,v_{k} \in \mathscr{A}, \; \alpha_{1},\alpha_{2},\dots,\alpha_{k} \in F \biggr\} \end{aligned}\] of all linear combinations is called the span of \(\mathscr{A}\).

A corner case The symbol \(\varnothing\) denotes the empty set, the unique set with size 0, and no elements.

By convention, \[\operatorname{Span}(\varnothing) = \{\vec{0}\}.\]

Spanning sets

Proposition 12 (cf. MT2501). Let \(V\) be a vector space and let \(\mathscr{A}\subseteq V\). Then \(\operatorname{Span}(\mathscr{A})\) is a subspace of \(V\).

Definition 13 (Spanning set.). A spanning set for a subspace \(W\) is a set \(\mathscr{A}\) of vectors such that \(\operatorname{Span}(\mathscr{A}) = W\). If \(\operatorname{Span}(\mathscr{A}) = W\), then for all \(v\in W\), we can write \[v = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \alpha_{i}v_{i} = \alpha_1v_1 + \alpha_2 v_2 + \cdots + \alpha_k v_k\] for some \(v_{1},v_{2},\dots,v_{k} \in \mathscr{A}\) and some \(\alpha_{1}\), \(\alpha_{2}\), …, \(\alpha_{k}\).

Example of a spanning set Suppose that \[\vec{e}_1 = \begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix},\quad \vec{e}_2 = \begin{pmatrix}0\\1\end{pmatrix}.\] Then \(\operatorname{Span}(\vec{e}_1, \vec{e}_2) = \mathbb{R}^ 2\).

Example 2.3.4(iii)Spanning sets If \(\mathscr{A}\) is a spanning set for a vector space \(W\), and \(w\in W\) is any vector, then \(\operatorname{Span}(\mathscr{A}\cup \{w\}) = \operatorname{Span}(\mathscr{A})\).

In other words, if you have a spanning set for a vector space \(W\), you can always add additional redundant vectors from \(W\) and the set will continue to span \(W\).

Example 2.3.4(iv)Spanning sets In the vector space \(F[x]\) (of polynomials over the field \(F\)) every polynomial can be written \[f(x) = a_{0} + a_{1}x + a_{2}x^{2} + \dots + a_{m}x^{m} \in \operatorname{Span}(1, x, x ^ 2,\ldots).\] Hence the set \[\mathscr{M} = \{ 1, x, x^{2}, x^{3}, \dots \}\] of all monomials is a spanning set for \(F[x]\).

Linearly independent vectors and bases

Definition 14 (Linear independence.). Let \(V\) be a vector space over a field \(F\). A set \(\mathscr{A}= \{ v_{1},v_{2},\dots\}\) is called linearly independent if the only solution to the equation \[\sum_{i=1}^{k} \alpha_{i} v_{i} = \alpha_1v_1 + \alpha_2v_2 + \cdots + \alpha_kv_k = \vec{0}\] (with \(\alpha_{i} \in F\) and \(k\geqslant 1\)) is \(\alpha_{1} = \alpha_{2} = \dots = \alpha_{k} = 0\).

If \(\mathscr{A}\) is not linearly independent, it is linearly dependent.

Linear dependence

Lemma 15 (Linear dependence lemma (cf. MT2501).). Let \(\mathscr{A}\) be a set of vectors in a vector space \(V\). Then \(\mathscr{A}\) is linearly dependent if and only if there exists \(v\in \mathscr{A}\) such that \(v\) is a linear combination of vectors in \(\mathscr{A}\setminus \{v\}\).

Lemma 16 (cf. MT2501). Let \(V\) be a finite-dimensional vector space. If \(\{ v_{1},v_{2},\dots,v_{m} \}\) is a linearly independent set of vectors in \(V\) and \(\{ w_{1},w_{2},\dots,w_{n} \}\) is a spanning set for \(V\), then \(m \leqslant n\).

Definition 17 (Basis, finite-dimensional, dimension.). Let \(V\) be a vector space over the field \(F\). A basis for \(V\) is a linearly independent spanning set. We say that \(V\) is finite-dimensional if it has a finite basis. The dimension of \(V\) is the size of any basis for \(V\) and is denoted by \(\dim V\).

Example 2.4.5 The set \[\mathscr{B}= \left\{ \vec{e}_1 =\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\\0\\\vdots\\0\end{pmatrix}, \vec{e}_2 =\begin{pmatrix}0\\1\\0\\\vdots\\0\end{pmatrix}, \dots, \vec{e}_n=\begin{pmatrix}0\\0\\\vdots\\0\\1\end{pmatrix} \right\}\] is a basis for \(V = F^{n}\). We shall call it the standard basis for \(F^{n}\). Hence \(\dim F^{n} = n\) (as you might expect).

Warning! We refer to the dimension of \(F ^ {n}\) as if it does not depend on the choice of basis. In fact it doesn’t which we will show later.

Example 2.4.6

The set \(\mathcal{P}_{n}\) of polynomials over the field \(F\) of degree at most \(n\): \[\mathcal{P}_{n} = \{ \, f(x) \mid \text{$f(x) = a_{0} + a_{1}x + a_{2}x^{2} + \dots + a_{n}x^{n}$} \ \text{for some $a_{i} \in F$} \, \}\] is a vector space (it’s a subspace of \(F[x]\)).

The set of monomials \[\{ 1, x, x^{2}, \dots, x^{n}\}\] is a basis for \(\mathcal{P}_{n}\) and \(\dim\mathcal{P}_{n} = n+1\).

Warning! We refer to the dimension of \(\mathcal{P}_n\) as if it does not depend on the choice of basis. In fact it doesn’t which we will show later.

Uniqueness of linear combinations

Lemma 18 (cf. MT2501). Let \(V\) be a vector space and let \(\mathscr{B}\) be a basis for \(V\). Then every vector in \(V\) can be expressed as a unique linear combination of the vectors in \(\mathscr{B}\).

Note that although the collection \(\mathscr{B}\) in Lemma Lemma 18 might be infinite, linear combinations are always finite.

2 out of 3

Theorem 19 (cf. MT2501). Let \(V\) be a finite dimensional vector space and let \(\mathscr{A}\) be a finite collection of vectors in \(V\). If \(\mathscr{A}\) satisfies any two of the following three conditions, then \(\mathscr{A}\) is a basis of \(V\) (and hence satisfies all three conditions):

  1. it is a spanning set for \(V\);

  2. it is linearly independent;

  3. it contains precisely \(\dim V\) vectors.

Reduction and extension to bases

Theorem 20. If \(\mathscr{A}\) is any collection of vectors in a vector space \(V\), and \(\mathscr{C}\) is a linearly independent subset of \(\mathscr{A}\), then there exists a basis \(\mathscr{B}\) for \(\operatorname{Span}(\mathscr{A})\) such that \(\mathscr{C}\subseteq \mathscr{B}\).

Corollary 21 (Reduction to basis (cf. MT2501).). If \(\mathscr{A}\) is a subset of a vector space \(V\), then \(\mathscr{A}\) contains a basis for \(\operatorname{Span}(\mathscr{A})\).

Corollary 22 (Extension to basis (cf. MT2501)). [prop-extension] If \(\mathscr{A}\) is a linearly independent subset of a vector space \(V\), then there exists a basis \(\mathscr{B}\) for \(V\) such that \(\mathscr{A}\subseteq \mathscr{B}\).

Dimension is well-defined

Lemma 23 (cf. MT2501). Let \(V\) be a vector space. Then any two bases for \(V\) have the same size and consequently \(\dim V\) is uniquely determined.
